What is Vacancy Tax? How does the Vacancy Tax affect me? How can I avoid paying the Vacancy Tax?



Across Australia, millions of people are experiencing overcrowded homes and unaffordable rent. The 2016 Census has revealed over a million dwellings (11.2% of housing) are unoccupied. In response to this, the government introduced a ‘Vacancy Tax/Fee’ as a solution to shift vacant homes into the rental market.

Australia-wide Vacancy Fee

On November 2017, the first vacancy fee was introduced across Australia. The law applies to all foreign persons who own Australian residential property that is neither occupied nor rented out for at least 183 days in a year. This includes:

  • Individuals who are not resident in Australia
  • Companies that are controlled by individuals who are foreign persons
  • Trusts that are controlled by individuals who are foreign persons.

The Vacancy Fee is the amount equivalent to the foreign investment application fee submitted to the FIRB for its approval to purchase the property.

Melbourne’s Vacancy Tax

On January 2018, Melbourne introduced a vacancy tax (1%) affecting homes in inner and middle Melbourne that were vacant for more than six months. In addition to the vacancy tax, Melbourne also imposed an absentee owner surcharge and land tax on vacant homes (1.65%). Potential liability for a homeowner in inner and middle Melbourne is up to 2.65% of their property’s value per annum.

ACT’s Vacancy Tax

Previously vacant homes in the ACT were exempt from the ACT Land Tax. However, starting from July 2018, the ACT Government had removed this exemption, introducing an indirect vacancy tax to every ACT homeowner with homes that are not occupied or rented out. Every homeowner with a vacant home in the ACT would now be charged 1% of their property’s unimproved value per annum.

Sydney’s Short-Term Restrictions

Sydney has not imposed any vacancy tax as of yet. However, on August 2018, a law was passed to restrict homes located in the Greater Sydney Region to a minimum occupancy of 180 days a year. It was announced that homeowners that fail to rent out their homes would face hefty fines up to $220,000.

Queensland’s Vacancy Tax Proposal

The Queensland’s Greens Party are advocating for a vacancy tax, imposing 5% tax on properties left vacant for more than 6 months.
